About Bubble Pop

My project explores the “No Text Characters” prompt by designing a UI that relies on visual and auditory cues. The entire experience is built around the concept of bubbles. Each click will generate a bubble and a corresponding sound. Upon clicking the generated bubbles, the bubbles will pop and, again, generate an appropriate sound.

The idea was sparked by the image of soap and its transformation into bubbles, which naturally suggested an interactive system where bubbles could be created and then pop. The design is intended to evoke a sense of fun and curiosity, much like blowing bubbles as a child.

Prototype Progress

The current prototype allows users to generate bubbles by clicking anywhere on the screen. Each bubble is created at the cursor’s location, assigned a random size, and linked with a random chosen musical note. The bubbles float upwards, fade out, and are eventually removed from the webpage.

At this stage, the interaction is deliberately simple so I could test the mechanics of bubble generation, animation, and audio. This helped me evaluate whether users would find the floating and fading satisfying, or whether a different animation style would be more engage (and necessary).

The prototype differs from the envisioned final project in several key ways: the bubbles do not yet pop when clicked, there is no option to reset the canvas, and colours are not yet randomised.

Technical Approach

I chose randomisation (Math.random()) as my advanced JavaScript technique. This technique felt the most natural for bubbles because it mirrors the unpredictability of their real-world behaviour.

The technique of randomisation is used in two key ways:

  1. To vary the size of the bubbles
  2. To vary the musical notes played

This creates a dynamic and engaging interaction where every click produces a slightly different visual and audio result. Later on, I will be implementing it to vary the colour of the bubbles produced as well. The location of the bubble generated will not be randomised as I believe it will not be an intuitive way of interacting.

Bubble Generation

The bubbles are generated using a createElement() function. A new element is created and styled via CSS, positioned at the cursor’s coordinates. Each bubble is given a randomised size through math.random(). Simultaneously, a random index is generated with both math.floor and math.random to select a note from an array, in which the audio is provided via Tone.js.

Feedback and Animation

The animation is handled though CSS transitions, which allows the bubbles to float upward and fade out. After the animation completes, a setTimeeout function removes the element from the page. This prevents the canvas from being overwhelmed with inactive bubbles, but for the final project I intend to keep bubbles onscreen until they are interacted with.

As I endeavour to randomise the colours of the bubbles generated, I may have to change the method in which the bubbles are generated, as JS is more suited for this purpose.

Reflection on Feedback

I asked 3 questions to evaluate the intuitiveness of the prototype. The questions are as following:

  1. Did you understand that clicking anywhere would generate bubbles?
  2. Did the look and movement of the bubbles match the sounds they made?
  3. Was the behaviour of the bubbles what you imagined would happen when you clicked?

Overall, users quickly grasped the main interaction, however, two key issues were discovered.

Sound Design

Although randomised notes made each bubble unique, some users felt the sounds did not match the appearance and behaviour of the bubbles. They described the tones as too much like a piano and suggested they should sound more “child-like”. Based on this, I plan to redesign the audio system so that the sound is linked more directly to the bubble’s characteristics. For example, larger bubbles will produce lower-pitched sounds and smaller bubbles will produce higher-pitched ones. This mapping should feel more intuitive and consistent.

Animation

Some users enjoyed seeing the bubbles float and fade away, but others expected them to pop or bounce around. This feedback confirmed my initial suspicion that fading out would not sustain engagement. For the final design, I plan to make the bubbles persist on the screen until clicked, at which point they will pop with a corresponding sound effect. If possible, I will also experiment with having the bubbles bounce gently around the screen, which may further increase the sense of play.

Final Thoughts

Through this feedback, I realised that my prototype demonstrated the technical feasibility of the idea but still needs further refinement. By adjusting the sound design and animation, I can better align the user’s expectations with the playful concept of bubbles.
